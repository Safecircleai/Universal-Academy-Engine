CHAPTER 1: COOLING SYSTEM OVERVIEW
=============================================================================

The heavy truck cooling system is a closed-loop thermal management system designed to maintain engine operating temperature within optimal parameters.

The primary function of the cooling system is to prevent engine overheating by dissipating excess combustion heat to the ambient environment.

Coolant, typically a mixture of water and ethylene glycol antifreeze, circulates through the engine block and cylinder heads to absorb heat from combustion.

The cooling system operates under pressure, which raises the boiling point of the coolant and improves heat transfer efficiency.

Engine coolant temperature should be maintained between 180°F and 210°F (82°C to 99°C) during normal operation.

Coolant level must be checked regularly in the overflow reservoir, not the radiator cap, to avoid scalding injuries.

A pressure-tested coolant system should hold 15 psi minimum for heavy-duty truck applications.

The cooling system contains several key components: the water pump, thermostat, radiator, fan clutch, coolant hoses, and overflow reservoir.

=============================================================================
CHAPTER 2: THERMOSTAT OPERATION
=============================================================================

The thermostat is a temperature-sensitive valve that regulates coolant flow between the engine and the radiator.

The thermostat remains closed during engine warm-up to allow the engine to reach operating temperature quickly.

When coolant temperature exceeds the thermostat opening temperature (typically 180°F to 195°F), the wax element inside the thermostat expands and opens the valve.

An open thermostat allows hot coolant to flow from the engine to the radiator for heat dissipation.

A stuck-closed thermostat causes engine overheating because coolant cannot circulate to the radiator.

A stuck-open thermostat causes the engine to run below optimal operating temperature, increasing fuel consumption and wear.

The thermostat bypass circuit allows a small amount of coolant to circulate through the engine even when the thermostat is closed, preventing hot spots.

Thermostat replacement is recommended every 100,000 miles or whenever a cooling system failure is diagnosed.

A dual thermostat system increases thermal regulation precision in high-performance and heavy-duty diesel engines.

CHAPTER 3: FAN CLUTCH DIAGNOSTICS
=============================================================================

The fan clutch controls cooling airflow through the radiator by modulating fan speed in response to thermal and hydraulic inputs.

A viscous fan clutch uses silicone fluid in a sealed chamber to couple the fan to the engine drive shaft.

When engine or ambient temperature increases, the bimetallic coil on the front of the fan clutch expands and opens a valve, allowing more silicone fluid into the working chamber.

More silicone fluid in the working chamber increases the coupling efficiency between the drive plate and the fan, causing the fan to spin faster.

A fully engaged fan clutch can consume up to 25 horsepower from the engine, making proper clutch operation critical for fuel efficiency.

Fan clutch failure modes include: seized (always fully engaged), freewheeling (never engages), and fluid leak (partial or inconsistent engagement).

A seized fan clutch causes excessive fuel consumption, increased noise, and premature fan belt wear.

A freewheeling fan clutch causes engine overheating at low speeds and during idle, especially under heavy load conditions.

To test a fan clutch, the engine should be warmed to operating temperature and then shut off. The fan should stop spinning within 3-5 revolutions; more than 5 free revolutions indicates a failed clutch.

Fan clutch replacement is required when the unit fails the spin-down test, leaks fluid, or exhibits bearing noise.

CHAPTER 4: FAILURE CASE STUDIES
=============================================================================

CASE STUDY A: THERMOSTAT FAILURE — OVERHEATING

A Class 8 semi-truck operating in summer conditions experienced sudden overheating. The driver noticed the temperature gauge rising above 220°F and pulled over immediately.

Diagnosis: The thermostat was found in the stuck-closed position. Coolant was unable to flow to the radiator.

Repair: The thermostat was replaced. The cooling system was flushed and refilled with fresh coolant mixture.

Outcome: After thermostat replacement, the engine maintained normal operating temperature of 190°F under load.

CASE STUDY B: FAN CLUTCH FAILURE — IDLE OVERHEATING

A medium-duty delivery truck experienced overheating only at idle or low speeds, but operated normally at highway speeds.

Diagnosis: The fan clutch was freewheeling. At highway speeds, ram air provides sufficient cooling; at idle, the fan must actively pull air through the radiator.

Repair: The fan clutch was replaced with an OEM unit.

Outcome: The truck no longer overheated at idle. Fuel efficiency also improved slightly.

CASE STUDY C: COOLANT LEAK — GRADUAL OVERHEATING

A fleet truck showed gradual coolant loss over several weeks. Temperature readings increased incrementally.

Diagnosis: A pinhole leak in a lower radiator hose caused slow coolant loss. As coolant level dropped, the system lost pressure and cooling capacity.

Repair: The radiator hose was replaced. The system was pressure-tested, flushed, and refilled.

Outcome: Coolant level stabilised and temperatures returned to normal.
